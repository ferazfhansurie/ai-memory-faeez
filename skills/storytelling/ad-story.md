# Ad Stories That Actually Work

## The mindset shift

A bad ad shows a product. A good ad shows a problem. **A great ad shows a person changing.** Your job is not to describe what the thing does — your job is to show who the viewer becomes after they buy it. Stop selling features. Start selling the *after*.

Two sentences you should be able to fill in before writing anything else:

> **Before:** my viewer is [feeling X] because [Y is broken in their life].
> **After:** my viewer is [feeling Z] because [the product fixed Y].

If you can't fill those in cleanly, you don't have an ad yet. You have a brochure.

## The four story spines (pick one — never mix)

### 1. Problem → Agitation → Solution (PAS)

Best for: pain-killer products. Tools, services, anything that fixes a clear annoyance.

> **Hook:** name the pain in one sentence. ("Your reels look amateur because you cut on the wrong frame.")
> **Agitate:** twist the knife. Specific, uncomfortable detail. ("You spend three hours editing and still get 200 views.")
> **Solve:** show the product doing the one thing that breaks the pain. Not features — the *moment of relief*.

### 2. Before → After → Bridge (BAB)

Best for: transformation products. Fitness, skincare, software that levels you up.

> **Before:** show the old life. Frustration, mediocre output, the protagonist looking lost.
> **After:** show the new life. Calm, winning, in control. Same person, different posture.
> **Bridge:** the product is the bridge. One line: "The thing that got me from there to here was X."

The trick: spend 70% on Before+After (the contrast), 30% on the Bridge. Most people get this backwards and spend 70% explaining the product.

### 3. Founder Confession

Best for: personal brands, indie products, anything where the founder is the marketing.

> **Confession:** "I built this because I was [embarrassed / broke / failing] at [specific thing]."
> **Stakes:** what was on the line if you didn't fix it.
> **Reveal:** the thing you built. Show it solving the original problem in real time.

This one only works if the confession is *specific and slightly embarrassing*. "I struggled with productivity" is not a confession. "I missed my own daughter's recital because I was still answering Slack at 8pm" is.

### 4. POV / Situation Comedy

Best for: brand awareness, top-of-funnel, products too abstract to demo directly.

> **Setup:** "When [oddly specific scenario]…"
> **Escalation:** the scenario gets worse, more relatable, more painful — usually 2-3 escalating beats.
> **Punch:** the product appears as the deadpan answer. No explanation. The viewer connects the dots.

## Hook engineering — the first 1.5 seconds decide everything

A hook is not a question. A hook is a **pattern interrupt** — something that contradicts what the viewer expected to scroll into.

The five hook types that work in 2026:

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Contradiction | "Everyone tells you X. Actually X is killing your Y." | "Stop posting daily. It's why your account is dead." |
| Specific number | A number that's weirdly precise | "I made $11,847 from one tweet. Here's what it said." |
| Mid-action | Open mid-motion, no setup | (Hands already chopping, sauce already pouring, camera already moving) |
| Confession | Something embarrassing or risky to say | "I lied on my LinkedIn for two years. Here's what happened." |
| Visual mystery | A frame that doesn't make sense yet | (Object falling, glitch effect, weird angle that pays off in 3s) |

**Things that are not hooks:** "Hi guys today I'm going to talk about…", a logo splash, a slow zoom on the product, a text card with the title.

## The single-emotion rule

Pick one emotion. Run the whole ad on it. The four that move people to buy:

- **Aspirational** — "I want to be like that."
- **Cathartic** — "Finally someone said it."
- **Curious** — "Wait, how does that work?"
- **Vindicated** — "I knew I was right about this."

Mixing emotions dilutes them. A funny + sad + epic ad lands as none of those. Pick one. The Before→After contrast in BAB is aspirational. The Founder Confession is cathartic. POV is curious or vindicated.

## Stakes — what's at risk if the viewer doesn't watch

Within the first 8 seconds, the viewer should know what they lose by scrolling. Not what they gain — what they *lose*. Loss aversion outpulls aspiration roughly 2:1.

Examples:
- Lose: "If you keep editing this way, you'll never break 1k followers." (specific, painful)
- Gain: "Get more followers with this tip." (generic, weak)

Always frame the stake in the negative first, the upside second.

## The transformation moment (the climax of the ad)

There is one specific frame in every great ad where the transformation lands. The "before person" sees the "after." A reaction shot. A look of relief, of surprise, of "oh."

That frame is your money shot. **Build the entire ad around it.**

If you can't identify which frame in your ad is the transformation moment, your ad doesn't have one — and that's why it feels flat.

## The CTA that converts vs. the one that doesn't

Bad: "Link in bio." "Check it out." "DM me."
Good: a CTA that names the *next emotion* the viewer will feel.

- "Tap the link and try it once. You'll laugh when it works on the first try."
- "Save this so you don't forget — you'll thank yourself next Sunday at 9pm when you're editing."
- "Comment 'GUIDE' and I'll send the breakdown. It's the one I wish I had two years ago."

Vague CTAs feel like begging. Specific CTAs feel like directing.

## How to apply this

When the user asks for an ad story / script:

1. Ask **what they sell** in one sentence. If they give two, push back — pick the most painful problem.
2. Ask **who the viewer is** — and specifically, what they were doing 30 seconds before this ad interrupts them.
3. Fill in the Before / After sentences out loud. Don't proceed if they're vague.
4. Pick **one** of the four story spines based on the product type. Justify the pick in one line.
5. Pick **one** emotion. Pick **one** hook type.
6. Write the script as: Hook (1.5s) → Story spine (the bulk) → Transformation moment (the money shot, mark the timestamp) → CTA (specific, names the next feeling).
7. Identify the transformation frame explicitly: "This is the shot you build the rest around."
8. Hand off pacing/cuts to `/reel-tempo` if the user wants it timed out beat-by-beat.

When critiquing an existing ad:

1. Find the Before and After. If you can't, that's the diagnosis — there's no story.
2. Find the transformation moment. If there isn't one, the ad has no climax.
3. Count emotions. If more than one is being attempted, pick the strongest and recommend cutting the others.
4. Check the hook against the five types. If it's "Hi guys today..." or a logo, replace it.
5. Read the CTA out loud. If it sounds like begging, rewrite it as a direction.
6. Give 3 edits ranked by impact. Lead with the one that fixes the story, not the one that polishes the surface.
